# Informational Interview Reference

Preparation for networking and informational conversations — not formal job interviews.

## What Is an Informational Interview?

A conversation with someone who works in a role, company, or industry you're interested in. The goal is to learn, not to ask for a job. These conversations build your network and give you insider knowledge that makes you a stronger candidate when you do apply.

## Generating Strategic Questions

Generate 5-6 questions based on:
- The user's target role and industry
- The user's background (from their resume)
- The relationship context (how they know this person)
- What information would be most valuable for their career decisions

### Question Progression

Structure questions from broad to specific:

1. **Ice-breaker / career path** — Easy, personal, builds rapport
2. **Daily reality** — What the work actually looks like
3. **Industry insight** — Broader trends and perspectives
4. **Specific role details** — Tactical information about the target role
5. **Career development** — Growth, skills, trajectory
6. **Networking closer** — Opens doors to further connections

### Required Categories

Every informational interview prep should include at least one question from each:

**Daily Responsibilities**
- "Can you walk me through what a typical day or week looks like in your role?"
- "What do you spend most of your time on that might surprise someone outside the field?"
- *Purpose:* Reality-check your assumptions about the role

**Career Development**
- "What skills or experiences were most important in getting to where you are?"
- "Looking back, is there anything you wish you'd done differently early in your career?"
- *Purpose:* Get actionable advice for your own path

**Growth Opportunities**
- "What does career progression look like in this field? What are the different paths?"
- "What skills are becoming more important in this space?"
- *Purpose:* Understand the long-term trajectory and stay ahead

### Relationship-Calibrated Questions

**Alumni (same school):**
- "How did our program prepare you for this role? Any gaps you had to fill?"
- "Were there any courses or professors that turned out to be especially relevant?"
- Leverage shared experience — it builds instant rapport

**LinkedIn connection (no prior relationship):**
- Keep questions more professional and industry-focused
- Don't ask overly personal questions
- Show you've done your homework: "I noticed you transitioned from [X] to [Y]..."

**Conference / meetup contact:**
- Reference the shared event: "After your talk on [topic], I was curious about..."
- Ask about the topic they presented on — they chose it because they're passionate about it

**Referral from mutual contact:**
- Mention the connection: "[Name] suggested I reach out because..."
- Ask what the mutual contact couldn't tell you (insider perspective)

## Strategic Purpose of Each Question Type

| Question Type | What You're Really Learning | How to Use It |
|---|---|---|
| Career path | Whether this trajectory matches your goals | Career planning decisions |
| Daily reality | Whether you'd actually enjoy this work | Role selection, interview answers |
| Industry trends | What skills to develop next | Skill development, resume keywords |
| Challenges | What to prepare for, red flags to watch for | Interview prep, realistic expectations |
| Advice | Tactical career moves | Immediate action items |
| Networking closer | Expanded network, potential referrals | Future opportunities |

## The Networking Close

Always end with a question that opens doors:

- "Is there anyone else you'd recommend I speak with to learn more about [topic]?"
- "Are there any communities, groups, or events you'd suggest for someone interested in this space?"
- "Would it be okay if I followed up with you in a few months as I progress in my search?"

**Why this matters:** The single most valuable outcome of an informational interview is often the second connection it leads to.

## Rules

1. **Generate questions specific to the context** — generic "tell me about your job" questions waste everyone's time
2. **Include the strategic purpose** for each question — the user should understand WHY they're asking it
3. **Calibrate formality** to the relationship — an alumni coffee chat is different from a cold LinkedIn outreach
4. **Never include questions about job openings or referrals** — that's not what informational interviews are for. If the person offers, great, but never ask
5. **Keep it to 5-6 questions** — these conversations should be 20-30 minutes, not an interrogation
6. **Include follow-up guidance** — remind the user to send a thank-you message within 24 hours
